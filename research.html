Working Papers

Compositional Difference-in-Differences for Categorical Outcomes

JMP
PDF
Abstract Many policy evaluations involve vectors of category-specific quantities, either through aggregation of categorical outcomes (e.g., employment type, major choice) or compositional measures (e.g., GDP by sector, votes by party, electricity generation by source). In such settings, beyond the quantities, treatment effects on both the within-category shares and the overall total across categories are often economically relevant. Standard approaches treat each category as a separate binary outcome and apply DiD independently. This often yields unrealistic counterfactual shares, fails to recover compositional effects and effects on totals, and lacks a clear economic interpretation. I develop Compositional Difference-in-Differences (CoDiD), a framework that jointly identifies treatment effects on totals and shares in an economically coherent way. The key assumption is parallel growth: absent treatment, vectors of log-quantities would have evolved in parallel across groups. Under a random-utility discrete-choice model, this is equivalent to parallel trends in expected utilities, so the average change in latent attractiveness is the same for treated and control units. Geometrically, counterfactual shares evolve along parallel paths in the probability simplex. For multiple periods, I also provide a relaxation that yields informative bounds. I illustrate the approach using the impact of early voting reforms on the 2008 U.S. election.

Compositional Synthetic Controls

PDF
Abstract Many policy evaluations involve vectors of category-specific quantities, arising either from the aggregation of categorical individual-level data (e.g., employment type, major choice) or from compositional measures defined at the aggregate level (e.g., GDP by sector, votes by party, electricity generation by source). In such settings, the shares of each category and the total across categories can each carry independent economic meaning. Practitioners typically apply the Synthetic Control Method to a single category of interest or to each category separately. The first approach discards information about the remaining categories and cannot identify the source of any compositional shift. The second produces a different set of donor weights for each category, so the implied counterfactuals do not correspond to a single hypothetical donor unit. This paper proposes compositional synthetic controls, a method that constructs a single synthetic counterfactual vector using one unified set of weights obtained by minimizing a distance between log-transformed quantities. Under a standard random utility framework, these weights admit a behavioral interpretation as measuring the similarity between the treated unit's representative agent and those of the control units in terms of preferences over alternatives. Geometrically, the implied synthetic shares coincide with the Fréchet barycenter on the probability simplex equipped with the Aitchison metric. I illustrate the method by studying the Colorado RPS policy on the electricity mix.

Identifying Treatment Effects on Categorical Outcomes in IV Models

arXiv
Abstract I develop a nonparametric framework for identifying treatment effects on unordered categorical outcomes using a binary instrumental variable. A local copula decomposition expresses each observed joint probability of outcome and treatment as a function of marginal potential-outcome probabilities and a Gaussian copula parameter encoding selection on unobservables. With a binary instrument, this system is underidentified by two degrees of freedom per outcome category. Under \emph{association similarity}, a new assumption requiring the copula parameter to be invariant across treatment states, the system becomes exactly identified and the full distribution of potential outcomes is nonparametrically point-identified. Two weaker alternatives, \emph{monotonic association} and \emph{bounded association}, deliver sharp partial identification bounds and a formal sensitivity analysis. I introduce a semiparametric IV multinomial logit model estimated by two-step maximum likelihood, prove $\sqrt{n}$-consistency, and conduct inference via bootstrap. Monte Carlo simulations confirm low bias and accurate coverage. An application to the Democratic Republic of Congo shows that improved market access reduces primary-sector employment by 21 percentage points and raises industrial employment by 14 percentage points.
